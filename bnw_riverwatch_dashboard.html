Hyde Park Lake — A Closer Look
The Persistent Problem

Hyde Park Lake — formed by damming Gill Creek in Niagara Falls — has experienced cyanobacteria blooms every year since 2017, the longest-running persistent bloom site in the watershed.


In 2021, BNW began testing for HAB toxins (microcystin & cylindrospermopsin) as part of a Bowling Green State University pilot project. Testing confirmed that microcystin levels more than doubled during peak bloom weeks and remained elevated as algae decayed — meaning the water stays dangerous even after a bloom visually clears.


BNW has deemed the lake unsafe for paddling from July onward in most seasons. Blooms have also disrupted outdoor education programming with neighboring school groups.

The Green Infrastructure Response

In 2022, BNW received EPA Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (GLRI) funding to design green infrastructure targeting two stormwater outfalls and golf course runoff entering Gill Creek above Hyde Park Lake.


Construction ran from spring to fall 2025. The completed project:

  • Captures and infiltrates over 2 million gallons of stormwater annually
  • Routes runoff through two new wetland filtration areas
  • Added meadow plantings, shoreline trees, and a lake aeration fountain
  • Reduces the phosphorus and nutrients that fuel cyanobacteria blooms

Monitoring and follow-up invasive species treatment ongoing through 2026. Partners: BNW and the City of Niagara Falls.

Understanding PFAS
What Are PFAS?

PFAS (Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a class of 15,000+ man-made chemicals that do not naturally break down — earning the name "forever chemicals." Used industrially to make products water, stain, and heat resistant, they accumulate in the environment and in human bodies.


Exposure has been linked to cancer, liver damage, decreased fertility, and immune system effects.

Exposure & Scale

Experts estimate that 200+ million Americans are exposed to PFAS through drinking water.


Nearly 30,000 facilities are estimated to discharge PFAS into surface waters across the US — currently without federal regulation under the Clean Water Act. Surface waters have no enforceable PFAS protections at the federal level.

Why BNW Tests Surface Water

Because surface water currently lacks federal PFAS protections, BNW began testing in 2022 to fill a critical data gap for the watershed. Results are publicly available and mapped interactively, with sites categorized by PFAS detection levels relative to the EPA's 2024 drinking water standard.


Visit: bnwaterkeeper.org/pfas-pfoa-pfos

2022 Pilot Findings: Cayuga Creek
What the First Year of Sampling Found

In the program's inaugural year, BNW focused initial testing on Cayuga Creek in Niagara County. Results were striking: 83% of all samples detected at least one PFAS compound, with multiple distinct PFAS chemicals identified in a single sample — illustrating that even one waterway can carry a complex mixture of these persistent chemicals. These findings helped build the case for rapid geographic expansion in subsequent years.

Conductivity — Most Widespread Concern

Conductivity measures dissolved ions — elevated levels signal road salt, agricultural runoff, sewage, or urban stormwater. It is the most consistently widespread water quality failure across the watershed. In 2025, BNW launched a dedicated Chloride Monitoring program to track road salt impacts more precisely.

pH & Dissolved Oxygen — Relative Strengths

DO stays consistently near zero exceedance — a positive sign for aquatic life. The 2022 pH spike to 10% may be linked to HAB activity, as dense algal blooms shift water alkalinity upward through daytime photosynthesis. pH returned to 3–4% in 2023–2025.

How Reporting Evolved: 2012–2018

Between 2012 and 2018, Riverwatch moved to a Water Quality Index (WQI) — a composite score (1–100) blending all four parameters, temperature, and site context into a single letter grade (A–F) per monitoring location, using the National Sanitation Foundation's calculator.


This approach was effective for communicating grades to the public, but the composite format makes direct parameter-by-parameter comparisons with 2011 or the 2019–2025 data difficult. WQI scores during this period generally ranged from Grade B to C across most sites.


Starting in 2019, BNW shifted to reporting watershed-wide percentage exceedance per parameter — a cleaner, more consistent format that makes year-over-year trends immediately visible. This is the format shown in the trend charts on this tab.

What Each Parameter Measures
Conductivity Most Widespread Problem

Conductivity measures how well water can pass an electrical current — a proxy for dissolved salts, ions, and pollutants. In WNY, elevated conductivity is strongly tied to road salt runoff, agricultural nutrients, and urban stormwater. It has been the most consistently problematic parameter since monitoring began — affecting 52–71% of samples every year since 2019.


NYS Standard: Samples shall not fall below the minimum or exceed the maximum threshold for the stream's designated class.

Turbidity Methodology Changed 2024

Turbidity measures how clear or cloudy the water is — how much suspended sediment, algae, or silt is present. High turbidity blocks sunlight, raises water temperatures, and smothers fish spawning areas. It was the worst-performing parameter in early years (87% exceedance in 2011). In 2024, BNW changed its visualization threshold from 5 NTU to 25 NTU — a reporting methodology shift, not a cleanup achievement.


NYS Standard: A single sample shall not exceed 5 NTU above natural background.

Dissolved Oxygen (DO) Strong Result

Dissolved oxygen is essential for aquatic life. Low DO kills fish and signals decomposing organic matter or elevated water temperatures. DO has been the best-performing parameter across the entire monitoring period — fewer than 1% of samples have fallen below the standard since 2019. Localized summer drops in smaller streams in 2011 remain a pattern to watch as climate change pushes water temperatures higher.


NYS Standard: No less than 6.0 mg/L for the Niagara River; 4.0 mg/L for Class C waters.

pH

pH measures water acidity or alkalinity (scale 0–14; 7 = neutral). Most aquatic life thrives between 6.5 and 8.5. pH has remained mostly within standard since 2019, with exceedance rates of 3–10%. The 2022 spike to 10% likely reflects HAB activity — algal blooms shift alkalinity upward through daytime photosynthesis. The return to 3–4% in 2023–2025 suggests no long-term deterioration.


NYS Standard: Between 6.5 and 8.5 for most freshwater classifications.
